Heritage · Art

Minor monuments and historic churches

Beyond its major landmarks, Montefiascone hides precious medieval and Baroque art jewels in its historical neighborhoods: explore Romanesque churches, ancient monasteries, and the grand gateways to the town.

Monuments Summary

Oldest Structure
Church of Sant'Andrea (mid-9th century)
Key Styles
Lombard Romanesque, Gothic, Roman Baroque
Accessibility
Fully accessible on foot from the centre

Church of Sant'Andrea in Campo

The Church of Sant'Andrea in Campo is one of the oldest and most historically significant buildings of worship in Montefiascone, first documented in papal bulls from 853 AD.

The characteristic tones of tuff, the millenary building material of the medieval village.

The current structure reflects an 11th-century Romanesque renovation carried out by Lombard stonecutters. It features a simple gabled facade made of grey peperino blocks, an arched portal topped by a single lancet window, and an interior divided into three naves supported by thick square pillars with carved capitals. Today, it hosts the local Orthodox parish.

Church and Former Convent of San Francesco

The Franciscan monastic complex has been documented in Montefiascone since 1291, built following the historical preaching of Saint Francis of Assisi on the hill in 1222.

In 1875, parts of the convent buildings were demolished and converted into the Civil Hospital on via Verentana. The church's interior, renovated in late Baroque style during the 18th century, houses outstanding artworks, including a rare 1490 panel painting of the Madonna and Child, as well as reliefs illustrating the saint's life.

Porta di Borgo (or Porta Aldrovandi)

Porta di Borgo is the main historical gateway into the medieval centre of Montefiascone, located on the ridge that connects the lower suburbs with the upper Rocca hill.

The arch of Porta di Borgo introduces visitors to the historic streets decorated for city festivals. Photo by Wine Fair.

The current Baroque facade dates back to a major 1744 renovation commissioned by Cardinal Pompeo Aldrovandi (often misspelled as Aldobrandini in popular accounts) and designed by the celebrated Roman architect Domenico Gregorini. The monument is characterized by curved travertine shapes and the cardinal's coat of arms placed at the top of the archway.

Church of San Pietro and Benedictine Convent

The Church of San Pietro, attached to the historic convent of Benedictine nuns in Montefiascone, is an important treasury of medieval painting in the Viterbo region.

The building preserves an outstanding series of 14th-century frescoes showing clear Sienese school influences, alongside Baroque altars and oil paintings from the 16th and 17th centuries by artists of the Viterbo school.

Walking Guidelines

These monuments are all situated within the pedestrian-only zone (ZTL) of the historic centre and can be easily visited on foot by following the local neighborhood signs.
